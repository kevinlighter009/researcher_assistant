# Dolphins: Multimodal Language Model for Driving

## Keywords
- vlm, openflamingo, grounded-cot, in-context-learning, bdd-x, video-instruction-tuning, conversational-driving

## TL;DR
Existing autonomous-driving stacks lack holistic scene understanding, instant adaptation, and error recovery, while general-purpose VLMs are not grounded in the AV domain and only handle static images. The authors build Dolphins on OpenFlamingo by (i) instruction-tuning with a Grounded Chain-of-Thought (GCoT) pipeline that elicits step-by-step reasoning over VQA images and (ii) constructing a 32k video-instruction-answer dataset over BDD-X covering four AV tasks (behavior understanding, behavior reasoning, prediction with control signals, detailed conversation). The resulting model demonstrates scene-level perception, prediction/planning rationales, few-shot adaptation, and reflection in a conversational interface; the paper is qualitative and reports no quantitative benchmark tables, only an inference-time measurement of 1.34 s on an A100.

## Problem & Motivation
Modular ADS pipelines are brittle on long-tail and dynamic scenes, and end-to-end designs lack interpretability. The authors identify three specific gaps versus human drivers: (1) holistic understanding/interpretation of complex open-world scenes, (2) instant learning and adaptation from a few examples, and (3) reflection and error recovery from feedback. Prior driving-oriented VLMs (HiLM-D, DriveGPT4, LINGO-1) suffer from low task diversity, often accept only a single video, and lack in-context-learning, which limits their generalization to unseen instructions. General LVLMs are aligned at coarse vision-language granularity, so they cannot perform the fine-grained spatial reasoning needed in AV scenes.

## Innovation Points
- **Grounded Chain-of-Thought (GCoT) instruction tuning** — a three-step ChatGPT-prompted pipeline (image content -> object/spatial grounding with bounding box -> reasoning + final answer) that augments general VQA datasets with grounded CoT responses to teach the VLM fine-grained step-by-step reasoning before AV adaptation.
- **AV-specific video-instruction dataset on BDD-X** — 32k video-instruction-answer triplets across four tasks (Behavior Understanding, Behavior Reasoning, Prediction with Control Signals, Detailed Conversation), with the Detailed Conversation split (~11k) generated by ChatGPT to elicit long-form, human-preferred answers.
- **Multimodal in-context instruction tuning for AV** — adapts the Otter/Flamingo in-context-tuning recipe with RICES (text-embedding-based retrieval) to select up to k=3 in-context exemplars per training triplet, preserving few-shot generalization in the driving domain.
- **Video-aware OpenFlamingo extension** — adds learned temporal position embeddings (time embeds) and learned media position embeddings on top of OpenFlamingo so the perceiver resampler can aggregate multi-frame features without losing global temporal ordering.
- **Two-stage training without a video-text alignment stage** — drops the conventional first-stage video-text alignment and instead fine-tunes first on image-instruction data with GCoT templates, then on the AV video-instruction dataset, transferring fine-grained reasoning from images to videos.

## Model Architecture
- Built on OpenFlamingo (re-implementation of Flamingo trained on Lion-2B + MMC4).
- Inputs: a driving video (multiple frames) plus a text instruction; supports multimodal in-context exemplars (image/video + instruction + answer triplets).
- Vision encoder: frozen CLIP ViT, applied per frame.
- Temporal/media position: per-frame features are augmented with learned **time embeds** and **media embeds**, then passed through a **Perceiver Resampler** to produce a fixed set of visual tokens.
- Text backbone: frozen LLaMA / MPT decoder, with **GATED XATTN-DENSE** layers inserted between LLM blocks (cross-attention from text to resampled visual tokens) and **LoRA** adapters on self-attention and feed-forward sublayers.
- Trainable parts only: perceiver resampler, gated cross-attention layers, and LoRA modules in the text encoder; CLIP and LLM weights stay frozen.
- Output: free-form text (scene description, behavior labels, predicted speed/turn-angle, multi-turn dialogue, etc.). Predicted control signals (e.g., next-second speed and turn angle) are emitted as text, conditioned on a history of textual control signals from BDD-X.
- In-context retrieval: VideoMAE for video embeddings and OpenAI text-embedding-ada-002 for text embeddings; cosine top-k retrieval (text-embedding only kept; k = 3) supplies few-shot exemplars at training/inference.
- Training data: image-instruction set with GCoT (built from public VQA + visual-instruction datasets and ChatGPT) for stage 1; ~32k BDD-X video-instruction-answer triplets (11k Detailed Conversation generated by ChatGPT, 21k from BDD-X labels) for stage 2. BDD-X provides ~7,000 videos / ~25,000 annotations.
- Optimization: AdamW (beta1 = 0.9, beta2 = 0.999, weight decay 0.01), DeepSpeed, 4 NVIDIA A100 GPUs, batch size 128, LR 2e-5 (stage 2), max sequence length 1024, BF16; loss is cross-entropy on answer tokens (between `<answer>` and `<endofchunk>`).
- Total parameter count: not reported.

## Benchmark Results
The paper presents a qualitative demonstration study rather than a quantitative benchmark; **no result tables (no metric numbers, no ablation tables) are provided**.

- Capability coverage (qualitative, Figures 5-27 / Sections 5.1-5.2):
  - Perception: scene understanding, ODD attributes (weather, time of day, illumination), traffic conditions, road-agent and ego-agent behavior — examples on BDD-X-style videos.
  - Prediction & Planning: VLM-based behavior prediction of other agents and ego future plans; the authors note these tasks are demonstrated via in-context learning, dialogue, and control-signal prompts because the BDD-X training data is limited.
  - Human-like capabilities: rapid adaptation via in-context learning (e.g., learning that "you cannot determine the time by the light in a tunnel" from a few shots), multi-turn interactive conversation derived from LINGO-1-style queries, and reflection / error recovery after user feedback.
- Quantitative measurement reported: **inference latency of 1.34 s per call on a single NVIDIA A100**, used by the authors to flag deployment cost.
- Headline metric on a closed driving benchmark: **not reported**.
- Ablations on GCoT, in-context tuning, time/media embeds, retrieval k: **not reported** numerically.

## Limitations & Open Questions
- No quantitative evaluation on standard AV benchmarks (e.g., BDD-X behavior-description metrics, nuScenes planning) and no comparison numbers against HiLM-D / DriveGPT4 / DriveLM; all evidence is illustrative figures.
- Inference latency 1.34 s on an A100 is too slow for control-rate driving and the authors acknowledge edge-deployment power consumption as an open issue; distillation is suggested but not done.
- Prediction and planning tasks rely on prompting (in-context examples, dialogue, hand-fed control signals) rather than supervised training, because BDD-X is limited; closed-loop or trajectory-level performance is unverified.
- Total model size, exact LLM backbone choice (LLaMA vs MPT) for the released model, and number of training tokens are not clearly stated.
- Hallucination is acknowledged in figure captions (highlighted "Words means hallucination"), but its rate and mitigation strategy are not quantified.
